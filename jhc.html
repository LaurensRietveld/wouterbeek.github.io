Daniel's conjecture: equal liberties + robustly equal opportunity + fair distribution of resources + support for our self-esteem --> eliminate to important degree injustices in health outcomes.

Basis for conjecture

  • Life expectancy in richer countries
  • Life expectancy between equally poor countries
  • Relative deprivation (both income and general)

Individual level: socioeconomic gradient: with each step higher up on the economic ladder we see improved health outcomes. The slope, however, is fixed by the level of income: the more unequal in economic terms, the more unequal in health terms.

Social mechanisms: income inequality --> educational inequality --> health inequality. Close correlation with dissolution of participation in social and governmental activity.

When are limits fair? Fair Process

  • Must nevertheless arrive at acceptable social policies: question becomes about legitimacy based on moral authority.

Justice as procedural fairness

    What are the features of a fair process?

  • Publicity Condition: rationales for decisions are publicly accessible to all affected by them.
  • Relevance Condition: Construal will be "reasonable" if it appeals to reasons and principles that are accepted as relevant by people who are disposed to finding terms of cooperation that are mutually justifiable.
  • Appeals Condition: There is a mechanism for challenge and dispute resolution regarding limiting decisions, including the opportunity for revising decisions in light of further evidence or arguments.
  • Enforcement Condition: There is voluntary or public regulation of this process to ensure that conditions 1-3 are met.

How does it work?

    Publicity Condition

  • Give 'defensible' arguments for why a treatment is or isn't included.
  • Case Law

    Relevance Condition: two constraints

  • a) Reasonable construal of how the organisation should provide value for money in meeting the varied health needs of a defined population under b) reasonable resource constraints.

    Enforcement Condition

  • There is voluntary or public regulation of this process to ensure that conditions 1-3 are met.

Explanation of two constraints:

  • Construal will be 'reasonable' only if it appeals to reasons that are accepted as relevant by 'fair-minded' people: people who seek mutuallt justifiable terms of cooperation.
  • Sports
  • Reasonable people will might disagree on a specific requirement associated with protecting opportunity, for instance allowing medical intervention for extreme shortness in otherwise healthy individuals.
  • In these cases of disagreement, what matters is the relevance of the reasoning involved.
  • Experts: for profit

Appeals and Enforcement

    Go beyond publicity requirements: challenge --> reconsideration

  • No direct input of clinicians or patients
  • The reasoning is made public, the public does not do the reasoning.
  • Anomalies: acceptable

Concluding

  • Extension of theory of justice to health care links previously separate questions 1 and 2: moral importance of health care and determining injustice of health inequalities through social correlation.
  • First establish a fair process to arrive at what is fair in rationing, since we lack consensus on the relevant distributive principles.
    • Secondly, principles of justice and the grounds for them must be publicly acknowledged.
  • Finally, public reason must constraint the content of public deliberation. No 'special considerations' based on 'comprehensive moral views' people hold. (aka: no n00bs in moral decision-making)

in order of increasing interestingness

  • Bureaucracy involved in the publicity of the decision-making process might lead to heaps of paper work us normal folks will never effectively read anyway. Practice of decision-making may still de facto be a 'black box'.
  • Appeal means that you can get others to delibertate again, you have no personal vote in the principles involved in decision-making: expert-entered and meritocratic: do we believe in special moral ability? and if not, is this method 'just'?
  • Theory assumes that other people (experts) know what is best for the public (you). Social contract is a contested notion, just because you were born 'here' doesn't not necessarily imply a whole set of positive duties.
  • Why not invest in finding effective ways to let people decide for themselves, instead of developing theories on how to let other people decide for them more effectively? Direct democracy --> faster and more autonomous solution to egalitarian problems.
